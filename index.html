The Limits of Cell Size

An interactive exploration of the surface area to volume ratio.

Why Does This Matter?

A cell is like a tiny factory. It needs to bring in raw materials (like oxygen and nutrients) and ship out waste products through its "loading docks"—the cell membrane (its surface). The factory's workspace is its volume. As a cell gets bigger, its volume (workspace) grows much faster than its surface area (loading docks). This tool shows why. Notice how the Surface Area : Volume Ratio gets smaller as the cell size increases. A smaller ratio means it's less efficient at moving materials in and out, which is why cells can't grow indefinitely!

Apply Your Knowledge

Let's apply this concept to some AP-style questions.

Question 1: The cells of a particularly large animal, such as a blue whale, are approximately the same size as the cells of a much smaller animal, like a mouse. Which of the following best explains this observation?

A) Larger animals require smaller cells to keep their overall body temperature down.

B) Smaller cells have a higher surface area-to-volume ratio, which allows for more efficient transport of nutrients and waste.

C) The cells of larger animals are more specialized and therefore do not need to be large.

D) The size of an animal's cells is determined by its genetic code, which is unrelated to its overall body size.

Explanation:

The correct answer is B. As you saw in the tool above, as a cell increases in size, its volume grows much more rapidly than its surface area. This decreases the surface area-to-volume ratio. A smaller ratio means the cell membrane cannot efficiently service the metabolic needs of the cell's larger volume. Therefore, both whale and mouse cells are kept small to maintain a high SA:V ratio, ensuring they can effectively exchange materials with their environment.

Question 2: Many plant roots have specialized epidermal cells called root hairs, which are long, thin extensions from the cell's main body. The primary function of root hairs is to absorb water and minerals from the soil. The structure of these root hairs is most effective for this function because:

A) The extensions decrease the overall volume of the cell, allowing for faster transport.

B) The extensions provide structural support to the root in the soil.

C) The extensions significantly increase the cell's surface area without a large increase in volume.

D) The extensions are able to store more water than a typical spherical cell.

Explanation:

The correct answer is C. The long, thin shape of root hairs is a classic evolutionary adaptation to solve the surface area-to-volume problem. By extending outwards, they dramatically increase the total surface area available for absorption. However, because they are so thin, this massive increase in surface area comes with only a very small increase in cellular volume. This results in an extremely high SA:V ratio, making them incredibly efficient at their job.

Question 3: Hereditary spherocytosis (HS) is a disorder of red blood cells that causes the cells to be smaller and spherical instead of having the usual flattened, biconcave shape. The average diameter of red blood cells in a person with HS was found to be 6.7 microns. The normal red blood cell has an average surface area of 136 microns² and an average volume of 91 microns³. Which of the following provides an accurate calculation of the surface area to volume ratio of an HS red blood cell, as well as a prediction of its effect on the efficient transferring of oxygen compared to a normal red blood cell?

A) The ratio is 0.45, and the cells are more efficient at transferring oxygen.

B) The ratio is 1.12, and the cells are less efficient at transferring oxygen.

C) The ratio is 0.89, and the cells are less efficient at transferring oxygen.

D) The ratio is 141, and the cells are more efficient at transferring oxygen.

Explanation:

The correct answer is C. Here is the breakdown:

  1. Calculate the radius: Diameter = 6.7 μm, so Radius (r) = 6.7 / 2 = 3.35 μm.
  2. Calculate Surface Area (SA) of the spherical cell: Using the formula 4πr², we get 4 * π * (3.35)² ≈ 141 μm².
  3. Calculate Volume (V) of the spherical cell: Using the formula (4/3)πr³, we get (4/3) * π * (3.35)³ ≈ 157.5 μm³.
  4. Calculate the SA:V Ratio for the HS cell: SA / V ≈ 141 / 157.5 ≈ 0.89.
  5. Compare efficiencies: The SA:V ratio for a normal red blood cell is 136 / 91 ≈ 1.49. Since the HS cell's ratio (0.89) is significantly lower than the normal cell's ratio (1.49), it is less efficient at the gas exchange required for transferring oxygen. The biconcave shape of a normal red blood cell is an adaptation that maximizes surface area for a given volume.
